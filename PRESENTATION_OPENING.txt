 VERSION A -- STANDARD OPENING  (60-75 seconds, recommended)
================================================================================

"Good [morning / afternoon], Professor, and thank you for having us.

[pause]

My name is [your name], and together with my teammates
Kodie Amo Kwame, Sumara Alfred Salifu, and Peta Mimi Precious,
we are presenting our final project for this course at RCSSTEAP,
Beihang University.

[pause]

Our topic is the prediction of Remaining Useful Life -- RUL -- of
aircraft turbofan engines, using physics-informed deep learning.

[pause, look up at audience]

Before I explain the method, let me ground it in why it matters.

A single commercial jet engine costs over twenty million dollars,
and more importantly, when one fails in flight, lives are at stake.
The entire discipline of Prognostics and Health Management, or PHM,
exists to answer one question:
    how many operating cycles does this engine have LEFT
    before it fails?

That number is called Remaining Useful Life, or RUL, and predicting
it accurately -- not optimistically, not late -- is one of the most
important open problems in aviation safety.

[pause]

In this project, we build and compare two neural-network models on
NASA's C-MAPSS turbofan degradation benchmark. The first is a
standard Convolutional Neural Network. The second is a Physics-
Informed Neural Network -- a PINN -- which bakes the physical
definition of RUL directly into its loss function.

[pause -- transition]

Our headline result, which I will defend in detail today, is this:

    the physics-informed model cuts Root Mean Square Error by
    twenty-one percent, and the asymmetric NASA score by almost
    seventy percent, WITHOUT adding a single convolutional layer.

Over the next few minutes, we will walk through the dataset, the
two architectures, the physics loss, the training, and the results.

[pause]

Let us begin with the dataset."

 VERSION C -- EXTENDED OPENING  (about 90 seconds)
================================================================================

"Good [morning / afternoon], Professor, and thank you for the
opportunity to present our work.

[pause]

My name is [your name]. My teammates are Kodie Amo Kwame, Sumara
Alfred Salifu, and Peta Mimi Precious. Together we form our project
group at the Research Centre of Satellite Technology and its
Applications -- RCSSTEAP -- here at Beihang University.

[pause]

Our final project investigates a question at the intersection of
aviation safety, machine learning, and physics:

    Can we predict, ahead of time, when a jet engine is about to
    fail -- and can physics-based reasoning make that prediction
    more trustworthy than pure data-driven deep learning?

[pause]

The quantity we are predicting is called Remaining Useful Life,
abbreviated RUL. In simple terms, RUL is the number of operating
cycles an engine has left before failure. Getting it right matters
in two directions. Predict too LATE and you risk an in-flight
failure. Predict too EARLY and you waste maintenance resources
replacing healthy hardware.

[pause]

To study this, we use NASA's Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion
System Simulation -- C-MAPSS -- which simulates fleets of turbofan
engines, each instrumented with twenty-one sensors, each flown to
failure. We focus on the FD001 subset: one hundred training
engines and one hundred unseen test engines.

[pause]

We built two models. A baseline one-dimensional Convolutional
Neural Network, trained only from sensor data. And a Physics-
Informed Neural Network, or PINN -- a simpler architecture, but
with two physical laws baked into its loss function:

   one, RUL must never INCREASE -- a degraded engine cannot
   un-degrade;

   two, RUL must decrease by exactly one cycle per cycle of
   operation, because that is the literal definition of RUL.

[pause]

The central finding of the project is that this second model, the
PINN, cuts Root Mean Square Error by twenty-one percent, and cuts
the asymmetric NASA scoring function -- which penalizes late
predictions four times harder than early ones -- by almost seventy
percent. It achieves this with fewer structural priors than the
CNN, which means the improvement is attributable to physics, not
to model capacity.

[pause]

Over the next three minutes, we will walk you through the dataset,
the two architectures, the physics-informed loss, training and
results, and finally interpretability -- what the network itself
learned about degradation.

[pause, look up]

Let us begin."


================================================================================
 DELIVERY TIPS
================================================================================

*   Start with your feet planted, hands relaxed. Look at the
    professor, not the slide.

*   Say "RUL" as the letters R-U-L, not as a word. Same for PINN
    (P-I-N-N), CNN (C-N-N), NASA (NASA is the only one pronounced
    as a word).

*   At every [pause] marker, count "one-thousand-one" silently.
    It feels long to you but sounds confident to the listener.

*   The line "twenty-one percent, and the asymmetric NASA score by
    almost seventy percent" is your money line. Slow down on it.
    Emphasize "seventy percent".

*   If you forget a word mid-sentence, DO NOT say "um" or "sorry".
    Just pause, take a breath, and continue. Silence reads as
    thinking; fillers read as unpreparedness.

*   Your teammates should nod in agreement during your intro --
    it signals to the professor that this is a group effort and
    builds cohesion.
